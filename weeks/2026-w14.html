⚠ ESCALATIONHormuz / Geopolitics13 Apr 2026
US announces Hormuz naval blockade after Iran talks fail — TTF swings back to €46/MWh
Following the collapse of US-Iran peace talks in Islamabad on 12 April, President Trump announced a US naval blockade of all vessels entering and leaving Iranian ports through the Strait of Hormuz from 13 April. The blockade re-escalated energy market volatility: TTF surged 6% back to €46.2/MWh on Monday before easing to the €42–43/MWh range on Tuesday as Iran signalled renewed willingness for dialogue. Reuters confirmed gas prices remain approximately 35% above pre-war levels. JPMorgan analysts noted that even if a ceasefire holds, damage to Gulf crude, petroleum and petrochemical production facilities will cause supply fluctuations for an extended period. The Atlantic Council described the Strait as experiencing an "effective shutdown" of one of the world's most critical commodity corridors.
STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS
→Re-escalation removes the partial relief from the 8 April ceasefire — European chemical feedstock and energy costs face a renewed upside risk trajectory
→JPMorgan: structural facility damage in the Gulf will cause supply fluctuations "even if ceasefire holds" — the floor for costs is durably higher
→Summer gas-storage refill at €42–47/MWh materially worsens European chemicals' H2 2026 cost baseline vs any pre-war scenario

EARNINGSSector-wide13 Apr 2026
European chemical firms face falling Q1 earnings — BASF, Brenntag, Evonik, Lanxess, Wacker all raising prices
Reuters reported on 13 April that European chemical companies are bracing for weaker Q1 2026 earnings as war-related disruption has sharply increased both energy and feedstock costs. VCI highlighted chemicals are hit harder than most sectors because they rely on oil and gas as both energy source and feedstock. Companies including Brenntag, Wacker Chemie, Lanxess, BASF, Evonik, EMS Chemie and Sika have raised prices — in some cases multiple times across different products. BASF's CFO said at a JPMorgan conference in March that pricing should more than offset cost inflation in Q2; Brenntag's CFO confirmed price increases are being accepted by customers. UBS and Deutsche Bank both flagged chemicals as among the most exposed sectors in the Q1 European earnings season.
STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS
→Price increases are being accepted in Q1 — but demand fragility means margin recovery sustainability into Q2 is uncertain
→BASF CFO's Q2 pricing confidence is the key near-term watch item — watch actual Q1 results and Q2 guidance language carefully

EXECUTIVE BRIEF — WEEK 10–16 APRIL 2026
European Chemical Sector: Geopolitical Re-Escalation Meets Q1 Earnings Season — Bifurcation Deepens Further

Overall Assessment: The collapse of US-Iran Islamabad talks and the announcement of a US Hormuz naval blockade on 13 April has re-escalated geopolitical risk just as Q1 2026 earnings season begins for European chemical companies. TTF remains elevated at €42–47/MWh — approximately 35% above pre-war levels — and the structural damage to Gulf energy infrastructure means costs will stay elevated through the summer refill season. Against this backdrop, European producers are pushing through multiple rounds of price increases, the EU is accelerating its electricity package, and specialty players continue to find growth in advanced materials, decarbonisation and application-differentiated innovation.

1. Hormuz Re-Escalation — US Blockade Adds New Uncertainty Layer
The US naval blockade of Iranian port shipping from 13 April — following failed Islamabad talks — has added fresh volatility to energy markets. TTF bounced back to €46/MWh before easing as Iran signalled dialogue willingness. JPMorgan analysts warned that structural facility damage in the Gulf will drive supply fluctuations even if a ceasefire is restored. The Atlantic Council described the strait as effectively shut down as one of the world's most critical commodity corridors. For European chemicals, this means feedstock and energy costs will remain elevated and volatile through H2 2026 regardless of diplomatic outcomes.
2. Q1 Earnings Season — Multiple Price Increases, But Demand Fragility Remains
BASF, Brenntag, Evonik, Lanxess, Wacker, EMS Chemie and Sika have all raised prices — some multiple times — to offset the cost shock. BASF's CFO has guided that pricing should more than offset cost inflation in Q2, and Brenntag's CFO has confirmed customers are accepting increases. However, analysts at Mwb Research and Deutsche Bank warn that demand remains weak and Asian competitors retain structural cost advantages. The Q1 results themselves — expected in late April and May — will test whether pricing discipline has actually protected margins.
3. EU Policy Package — Most Comprehensive Response Yet
The EU's 14 April draft package — electricity-tax cuts, coordinated gas-storage filling and an electrification target — is the most coordinated crisis-policy response to date. Combined with the newly launched REsourceEU critical minerals platform, it signals that European industrial policy is shifting from reactive relief discussions toward structured, multi-instrument coordination. For chemicals, electricity-tax cuts and storage coordination are the most immediately relevant levers. The electrification target matters for longer-term process investment planning.
4. Specialty Signals — Growth Continues in Advanced Materials, Digital Infrastructure and Decarbonisation
Three specialty signals stand out: Nouryon's colloidal silica capacity expansion (catalysts/advanced coatings), Arkema's data-center advanced materials positioning, and BASF's industrial heat-pump execution at Ludwigshafen. These reinforce the core bifurcation thesis: European chemical investment is concentrating in higher-value, application-differentiated, and decarbonisation-linked segments while base chemicals remain under acute pressure.
