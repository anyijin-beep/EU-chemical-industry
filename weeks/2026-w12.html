⚠ CRISISGlobal / Hormuz26 Mar 2026
Iran war chokes petrochemical supply — plastic prices hit 4-year highs, increases up to 50%
Reuters reported that the Iran war significantly disrupted petrochemical and oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz, sending plastic prices to four-year highs. The Middle East accounted for more than 40% of global polyethylene exports in 2025, making Europe particularly exposed through both supply availability and naphtha-linked cost escalation. Some producers were already pushing through price increases of up to 50%. Qatar confirmed it can no longer honour LNG contractual obligations following attacks on Ras Laffan Industrial City — repairs could take up to five years. Goldman Sachs raised its Q2 2026 TTF forecast to €72/MWh; an adverse scenario could push Summer 2026 TTF above €89/MWh.
STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS
→Upstream and polymer margins remain hostage to imported feedstocks and geopolitics — further rationalization imminent
→Competitive advantage widening for lower-cost regions (US, Middle East ex-Iran) vs. European naphtha-based producers
→If disruption persists, will reinforce rationalization pressure and potentially accelerate capacity exits in Europe

EXECUTIVE BRIEF — WEEK 23–30 MARCH 2026
European Chemical Sector: External Shock Compounds Structural Weakness — Crisis Mode Confirmed

Overall Assessment: The week of 23–30 March marks an escalation from structural pressure to active crisis. The Iran war's disruption of Strait of Hormuz petrochemical flows has delivered an external shock on top of already-stressed European chemical economics. TTF gas has surged ~70% in March to ~€55/MWh (vs €38/MWh at the start of the month), plastic prices have hit four-year highs, and both labor (IGBCE wage deferral) and industry (paused projects, frozen hiring) are signaling that recovery remains distant.

1. Hormuz Shock — Feedstock and Logistics Crisis Now Acute
The Iran war has disrupted more than 40% of global PE export flows and inflicted potentially multi-year damage on Qatar LNG supply (Ras Laffan). Plastic price increases of up to 50% are already being pushed through. Goldman Sachs has raised its Q2 TTF forecast to €72/MWh with an adverse scenario above €89/MWh for Summer 2026. This is not a temporary spike — Qatar LNG repair timelines of up to five years mean the supply structure has changed.
2. German Chemical Sector — Labor Agreement Signals Structural Defensiveness
IGBCE's agreement to defer wage hikes to January 2027 is a meaningful structural signal — labor and management are both pricing in a prolonged weak environment. Combined with VCI's stagnation forecast and paused capital projects across BASF, Lanxess, and peers, Germany's chemical base is clearly not positioned for near-term recovery.
3. Policy Response — Targeted Relief, Not Structural Fix
EU ministers are pursuing targeted relief through ETS revenues, limited tax cuts, and efficiency measures — explicitly rejecting broad subsidies. This approach helps at the margin but does not solve Europe's underlying energy cost disadvantage. Borouge Group International's imminent closing meanwhile signals that global polyolefin consolidation is accelerating, with implications for European asset valuations.
4. Innovation Signal Remains — BASF PCF Positioning Continues Under Crisis
BASF's 23 March announcement on renewable-electricity-produced acid chlorides and chloroformates (avg 19% PCF reduction across ~25 products) shows that lower-carbon specialty positioning continues as a competitive lever even in a crisis environment. TotalEnergies' net-zero reassessment is the counterpoint — headline decarbonization ambition is retreating, but product-level PCF differentiation is holding.

→Borouge Group International actual closing and first integration signals — polyolefins competition implications for Europe
→Fresh closure, rationalization, or restructuring announcements from major producers following IGBCE deferral signal
→Avantium PEF/FDCA commercial traction — bellwether for Europe's ability to industrialize bio-based polymer platforms
